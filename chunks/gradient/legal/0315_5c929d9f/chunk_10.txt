As Rahel Jaeggi 2018, p. 36 explains, all human societies constantly try to solve problems that arise due to social crises, conflicts, and contradictions, and to the extent that these acts of problemsolving do not involve moments of regressionextending from ideological distortions to alienation, and blockages to collective action they may be considered progressive developments.8 Identifying moments of regression, in turn, requires a sustained analysis of the internal inconsistencies and contradictions of the respective problemsolving processes and their outcomes. This way of conceptualizing progress has particular merit when thinking about democratic backsliding as a regressive phenomenon that may occur in various different cultural contexts.
